Deep brain stimulation (DBS) is an invasive neuromodulation technique targeting deep brain nuclei with the aim for modifying brain activity. Due to side effects such as infection, haemorrhage, headache, or seizure, DBS treatment availability is limited to patients with treatment-resistant depression. Compared to earlier lesioning procedures (e.g. limbic leucotomy) DBS shows superiority in that it is adjustable, non-destructive, and is reversible. Although DBS is a promising treatment in the management of treatment-resistant depression, due to insufficient knowledge on its mechanism, effectiveness, and safety, careful considerations need to be taken when recommended for the clinical management of psychiatric disorders and should only be applied in patients who fully understand the risk and benefit of this procedure.
Crucial ethical points when recommending DBS are autonomy and well-being of patients, altering personal identity, justice (e.g. priority to the most affected patients), clinical trial design, and cost and resource allocation. Major issues affecting patient`s autonomy, such as biographical disruption, control of human personality, and concerns about informed consent given by depressed patients have raised significant ethical debates (Bewernick and Schlaepfer, 2015). Further research in the investigation of the influence of patient characteristics, level of resistance, and the optimal DBS settings on the outcome of the treatment is needed.
